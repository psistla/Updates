
## 1. Set up the conversation

Ask for a short, dedicated 1:1 rather than raising it off‑the‑cuff.

You could write:

> “Could we set up 30 minutes sometime next week to talk about my growth and promotion path? I’d really value your feedback and perspective.”

This frames it as development, not a complaint.

## 2. Open with intent and appreciation

You want to make it clear you’re not attacking them or the process.

Example opening:

> “Thanks for taking the time to talk. I want you to know I’m committed to the team and to doing great work here. I’d like to understand more clearly what’s holding me back from being considered for promotion so I can work on the right things.”

This:
- Signals cooperation.
- Centers learning and clarity, not blame.
- Keeps the tone professional, not emotional outburst.

## 3. Briefly restate your case, without re‑litigating

You’ve already sent the document; here you just reference it and focus on alignment.

> “I put together that document outlining my contributions and how I’m meeting the metrics for the next level. From my perspective I’m hitting the criteria we discussed. I’d really appreciate your view on where you see gaps or concerns.”

Key points:
- Use “from my perspective” to avoid sounding absolute.
- Explicitly invite their view.

## 4. Ask targeted, non‑defensive questions

Instead of “Why am I not being promoted?” use questions that open dialogue and get specifics:

- “When you think about people at the next level, what are the behaviors or impact you expect to see day‑to‑day?”
- “Where do you see me relative to that? Are there specific examples where I didn’t demonstrate what’s needed?”
- “If you had to name the top two things that are blocking my promotion right now, what would they be?”
- “What would ‘ready’ look like in a way that we could both recognize it?”

If they stay vague, gently push for concreteness:

> “That’s helpful. To make sure I’m focusing on the right things, could we translate that into a few specific expectations or examples so I know what to do differently?”

## 5. Co‑create a clear plan and timeline

You want to leave with something you can act on, not just general feedback.

- “Would you be open to setting 3–5 concrete goals that, if I achieve them, you’d feel confident supporting my promotion?”
- “What timeframe feels realistic for revisiting this formally?”
- “How will we track progress so neither of us is surprised when we talk about promotion again?”

After the meeting, send a short follow‑up email:

> “Thanks again for talking today. Here’s my understanding of what I’ll focus on: [bullet your 3–5 goals]. Let me know if I missed anything. I’ll check in on this again around [month].”

This makes it harder for the expectations to shift or disappear.

## 6. Acknowledge your feelings without making it their fault

You can be honest about the mental toll while staying professional.

Something like:

> “I also want to be candid that the uncertainty has been tough for me. I care a lot about growing here, and not knowing where I stand has been mentally draining. I’m sharing that not to blame anyone, but so you understand why clarity and a path forward matter a lot to me.”

This:
- Names the pain and honors it.
- Avoids accusations (“you” statements).
- Reinforces that you’re looking for clarity, not sympathy.

If you’d rather be even softer:

> “Emotionally, the waiting and not knowing has been challenging. Getting concrete feedback and a clearer path would really help me stay focused and motivated.”

## 7. Protect the relationship and your dignity

During the conversation:
- Stay curious more than persuasive: ask, then listen, then paraphrase what you heard.
- If you disagree, say “I see it differently, can I share why?” instead of arguing.
- If feedback stings, say “Thank you for being direct; I’ll sit with this and think through how to apply it.”

Afterwards, evaluate:
- Did you get specific, actionable feedback?
- Did they commit to measurable steps and a timeframe?
- Or did you get vague, moving‑target answers?

If, after one or two of these conversations, you still get no clarity or forward movement, that’s useful data that you may need to explore options elsewhere. But that’s a separate decision you can take on your own timeline, without making threats in this conversation.
